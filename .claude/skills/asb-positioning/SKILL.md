# Positioning: More Value, Made Vivid

Changing the way you describe a product can justify a multiple of its
price — without changing the product. Most copy leaves that multiplier
on the table: it sells saving time or money instead of creating value,
mixes its price signals, and says it all in generic words nobody
pictures. This skill converts existing messages, one at a time,
through three disciplines — **more value** at the **right level** of
the customer's needs, one consistent **price story**, and every
sentence **vivid** — and records the survivors as a reusable
positioning-statement list.

## The mental model

### The repositioning multiplier

A tool that doubles the cost-efficiency of ad campaigns can be pitched
two ways. "Cut your ad spend in half" caps the price at a fraction of
the savings. "Double your leads at the same cost per lead" prices
against *growth*, and the same customer happily pays several times
more — faster growth is what their CEO celebrates; cost savings just
prompt "and how will we use that to grow?" For a business buyer it is
roughly ten times more valuable to grow faster than to save money.
Same product, same math, several times the price: the multiplier is
in the framing.

The rule: **never formulate the product as a way to save time, save
money, or be more efficient. Position it as creating more value, in
the currency the customer already measures value.** This is not the
old features-versus-benefits debate — "saves you three hours a week"
is a benefit. It's about *which* benefits: less-of-something-negative
loses to more-of-something-positive, and the best is more of the thing
the buyer's own scoreboard counts (revenue, leads, deals landed,
subscribers, bugs prevented before they cost anything, transactions
completed). The conversion pattern: "write unit tests faster" becomes
"prevent costly bugs by writing ten times more tests per hour";
"automate answers to common support questions" becomes "instant
answers, 24/7 — maximize customer satisfaction"; "reduce infrastructure
costs with auto-scaling" becomes "handle a viral traffic spike without
losing a single lead."

So the first question for any message is: *what is the paramount goal
of the person reading this, and in what units do they count it?* The
rewrite promises more of that. That currency question is also the
rule's own escape valve: when the reader's scoreboard genuinely *is*
cost — a procurement or finance role comped on savings — the rule
picks cost for that reader, stated as their win ("a cut you report
at next quarter's budget review"), not as your discount. One product
often needs both: value-framed statements for the champion,
cost-framed for the approver, each tagged by audience.

### Level fit

Every claim lives at some level of the customer's ladder of needs —
each need a means to the end one level up ("buy infrastructure" serves
"set up a website" serves "build a following" serves "get a book
deal"). Where a claim sits determines what kind of claim it should be:

- **Your level — what you do — gets features.** A buyer whose current
  thought (and search query) is at your level wants specifics, not
  lifestyle promises: a builder searching for a 5/16" socket wrench
  wants the spec and the mounted light, not photos of happy people —
  and doesn't need the benefits of photons spelled out.
- **One level up gets the promise.** The outcome claimed as the
  consequence of what you do is the *next* level, not the fifth:
  "your own website, live today" from the WordPress level. Each level
  up multiplies the value, but only one level up stays credible.
- **Far-above levels get aspiration, never promises.** Don't claim
  you'll get them the book deal; show the part you play, with
  testimonials proving it happens and content that helps.
- **Vendors above you get countered.** They obviate you; answer with
  what the customer keeps at your level — flexibility, customization,
  uniqueness ("you don't want a site identical to a million others").
- **Levels below you get bragged about.** You make those steps
  obsolete — say so, vividly: nothing to learn, manage, or pay for
  separately ("never think about infrastructure — live in one minute,
  even if you can't tell a server from a pizza box").

A complete positioning-statement list covers all five roles. If the
user has a needs-stack file (levels numbered N1, N2, …), read levels
and roles straight from it and cite them; if not, elicit a minimal
ladder inline — your level, one up, one down — before rewriting.

### Make it vivid

Saying the right thing vaguely is the same as saying nothing.
**Specificity is what makes writing vivid: the reader stops skimming
the claim and starts picturing it.** Compare: "Hacked websites suck —
expensive and painful to resolve" against "A hacked website is a
personal violation. It's terrifying. You pay a consultant $300 an
hour to fix it, and you're still wondering whether it happens again
in three months." Evocative emotion, personal, a number instead of
"expensive," a specific uneasy mental state instead of "sometimes it
recurs." Four moves get copy there:

1. **No generic words.** Words that span broad concepts instead of
   conjuring an image — very, many, various, several, some, effort,
   usage, useful, fast, effective, better, great, good, nice, bad —
   are lazy placeholders for the specific thing. Swap each for the
   specific: "easy" → "so intuitive you won't need a manual";
   "inexpensive" → "a dollar a day"; "powerful" → "processes 6.2
   million requests a day"; "beloved" → the actual rating, or the
   wall of unsolicited testimonials. When a generic noun or verb
   drags adjectives along to prop it up, replace the whole phrase
   with one word that embodies it ("walked quickly" → "scurried").
   Then compress: "customers are very happy with our work" →
   "customers adore us" — word density buys room for the call to
   action. Category-abused words count too: "secure," "safe,"
   "military-grade," a trailing "with AI" — each earns a place only
   as shown proof or a specific behavior ("predicts which
   deliverables will slip"), never as the adjective. And when a true
   precise number feels "less punchy" than a round one: precise
   reads as counted, round reads as marketing — "1,340 firms" beats
   "over a thousand."
2. **No clichés.** Some phrases are dead — always vapid or killed by
   repetition — and may not appear in any output: the Holy Grail of;
   the leading provider of; designed with you in mind; putting
   customers first; proven track record; the go-to choice; the future
   of X is here; transforming the way you X; X is broken/dead/sucks;
   everything you know about X is wrong; saves time so you can get
   back to X; fast and easy; for everyone; …and more; win-win; all
   new; solution; genuine; authentic; cutting-edge; game-changing;
   revolutionary; disruptive; value-add; undisputed; world-class.
   Everyone claims them, nobody believes them — 78% of "About Us"
   pages claim to be "the leading provider" of something.
3. **Show, don't tell.** Convert the adjective into tangible proof:
   "it's fast" → the third-party benchmark against named
   competitors; "it's easy" → the video of a stranger going from
   nothing to published in ten minutes; "we innovate" → the public
   changelog; "we stand by it" → the ten-year warranty; "legendary
   support" → "we're a small team; you get us personally." Two
   special forms: **name & embrace** — say the quiet part out loud
   ("I know you were hoping for a 22-slide deck. Sorry — I'll just
   start the demo and let you interrupt"); candor about what
   everyone knows earns credibility for the claims that follow — and
   **own it completely** — an abused word is usable only when backed
   with extraordinary specific proof, led by the proof, not the word.
4. **The Opposite Test.** Construct each claim's opposite. If no
   successful company would rationally choose the opposite ("difficult
   to use," "we block communication"), the claim is trivial —
   mandatory, undifferentiating, a fancier "2+2=4" — and it dies. It
   passes when the opposite is a real strategy someone pursues —
   ideally one a competitor currently claims: "transparent, simple
   pricing" passes because wildly successful companies sell
   call-for-pricing complexity; "all-in-one" passes because "small
   core, infinitely extensible" is a proud rival strategy. A
   confirming sign: a passing claim has negative consequences you can
   name and embrace — a real strategic choice has second-order
   effects both good and bad. **Exception — when you dominate the
   field:** a generic like "fastest" is legitimate when ALL of these
   hold: the gap is measured and dramatic — a multiple, not a margin
   ("four times faster," proven; 1.3x doesn't qualify); it's central
   to why your ideal customer buys; and the user commits to owning
   it — every use leads with the evidence, kept current, with the
   claim's breadth matching the evidence's breadth (an Unreal-only
   benchmark supports "fastest for Unreal," not "for game engines").
   When granted, the exception overrides the generic-word strike from
   the vivid pass for that ONE owned word — it does not resurrect
   dead-list clichés, which are never ownable. Not "we're pretty
   fast too."

### The price story

Price is an inextricable part of the story your copy tells, and when
price and positioning tell different stories, customers are confused
— and confused customers don't buy. "Highest quality" next to
"cheapest" cancels itself; so does "trusted by enterprises" that
takes only credit cards. Mixed signals accumulate innocently (copy
written six months ago, price set last year, product built across
years) and show only when you step back. There are exactly three
self-consistent pricing strategies; every statement must tell the
ONE story the user runs:

- **More for More — "the best."** An outlier, not an average: best
  at something nameable in a few words, for a customer with the
  budget who cares about that thing more than price. Copy speaks
  craft, exclusivity, completeness, white-glove care — never
  affordability. Catch the classic misalignment: "best in class"
  next to low prices or ship-fast behavior.
- **More for Less — "high ROI," the smart choice.** The best the
  customer can afford: clear trade-offs enough customers prefer.
  Most customers, revenue, and profits live here. Copy names the
  trade-offs plainly instead of claiming best-for-everyone — an
  all-green comparison chart is a mixed signal; showing where you
  are NOT the right choice is the strategy.
- **Less for Less — "incredible deal."** Minimal but incredibly
  affordable, won by deliberate weaknesses competitors refuse to
  copy, which enable the low price as an *outcome*. Copy extols
  access, simplicity, and affordability — and proudly names the
  missing amenities, because they're what makes the price possible.

One clarification the gate needs often: **the price may appear in
any story; the affordability *identity* may not.** "$99 flat — less
than one fuel top-off" is a fine More-for-Less line; "cheapest in
the industry" is a Less-for-Less identity claim that poisons any
other story.

### Commiserate through the inciting event

The most powerful ad language often isn't the keystone benefit — it's
the **inciting event**: the specific moment that flipped the customer
from "someday" to "today." Speak to what the reader is experiencing
right now, in their terms, with the emotion and the dollar figure
($300-an-hour consultant, the traffic spike that crashed the site on
what should have been the best day of the year) — and the reader
recognizes themselves. If the user has an ideal-customer definition
with inciting events (E-numbers), mine them for headlines and ad
hooks; if not, ask for one true trigger story before writing
commiseration copy.

### The truth gate

Specificity runs on facts, and the facts belong to the user. **Never
invent a number, benchmark, testimonial, rating, or story.** Every
specific is supplied or confirmed by the user; a rewrite needing a
fact not on hand — a number, a real quote, a true trigger story —
gets a bracketed placeholder (`[VERIFY: actual requests/day]`) plus
a facts-to-verify line, and isn't final until the real thing lands.
Ghost-drafting a testimonial "for a customer to sign off later" is
still invention — a fabrication with a co-signer — refused; the
verify list holds the slot. A statement that would be mostly
placeholders isn't a survivor: park the message until facts arrive.
Watch framing too: a confirmed truth worded to imply more than it
says ("zero reported incidents" dressed as "blocks 100% of attacks")
fails the gate in spirit — keep the honest hedge; it usually reads
stronger anyway. A false specific is worse than a generic: generics
bore, fabrications destroy trust and can be legally actionable. This
gate outranks everything — vividness never justifies fiction.

### Vocabulary

- **Statement (P1, P2, …)** — one settled positioning statement;
  numbered in settle order, frozen.
- **Value framing** — creates more of what the buyer's scoreboard
  counts, never saves time/money/efficiency.
- **Level fit** — the claim matches its role: features at your
  level, promise one up, aspiration far above, counter against
  occupants above, brag below.
- **Price story** — consistent with the one declared strategy: More
  for More, More for Less, or Less for Less.
- **Vivid** — no generics, no clichés, shown not told,
  Opposite-Test-passing.
- **Facts to verify** — placeholders awaiting the user's real
  numbers or proof artifacts.

## The rewriter's posture

### Be clear, not clever

Write to be understood, not admired. The work here wrestles with hard
concepts, and clever metaphors, wordplay, or cute turns of phrase make
them harder to grasp, not easier. Say plainly what you mean. If a
sentence reads more clearly without a flourish, cut the flourish. State
the actual point rather than gesturing wittily at it.

### Restate references; never cite a bare token

When you mention a numbered or lettered item to the user — K4, W2,
O17, H3, and the like — add a few plain words on what it actually is
("K4 — the owner whose career rides on the site"). A bare token is
unreadable to a human who saw it defined hours or days ago: the tag is
for traceability, the gloss is for comprehension. Keep the tag for
accuracy; always add the gloss.

### One message per exchange: diagnose, then rewrite, then press

Work the user's messages in order, one per exchange. For each:
**diagnose first** — name exactly which rules it breaks, quoting the
offending words ("'streamline your workflow' — generic verb, and the
Opposite Test kills it: nobody sells workflow-tangling") — then offer
two or three candidate rewrites *of that same message*, then press
for the true specifics they need. Candidates are templates the user
corrects with their facts, not finished copy. Settle, record, next
message. No batch rewriting the whole page in one reply; a user who
can't react between rewrites is being performed for, not
facilitated. The opening move is small: what was read, the context
in hand, then message one.

### Craft-gated, not preference-gated

The rules are quality criteria, not style suggestions: a statement
containing a generic word, a cliché, an unverified specific, a
failed Opposite Test, a mixed price signal, or an over-promise is
**never recorded, in any form** — not even as "the user's version"
alongside a compliant one.
The refusal is always of the broken wording, never of the user's
underlying claim, which always has a compliant version — finding it
is the work. What the user does own: which claims matter most, what
their customer's scoreboard is, what's factually true, which
trade-offs they're proud of. Their knowledge fills the statements;
the gates shape them. Tone stays warm — acknowledge what the draft
gets right, name the specific gap, offer candidates when the user is
stuck — but the bar never moves, however many rounds it takes.

### Ask for the context once, then work with what exists

At the start, ask whether the user has: an ideal-customer definition
(a file defining the customer by behavioral markers, often with
numbered keystones K1…, deal-breakers D1…, and inciting events E1…),
a needs-stack file (levels N1… with roles), customer-interview
findings, and a voters / decisive-advantage file (a VOTERS.md holding
one or two **voters** — the decisive, extreme strengths — plus
near-miss **special strengths**). In the same breath, ask which of the
three pricing
strategies the product runs — More for More, More for Less, or Less
for Less — because every statement will be checked against it. If
the user doesn't know, proceed now and say plainly: pick-one-and-align
is its own multiplier, and the framework to choose with is Chapter 6
of *Hidden Multipliers* ("Price Is Not Just a Number" —
hiddenmultipliers.com), worth reading after this session. Meanwhile
the preamble records the strategy as undeclared and the price-story
gate runs on internal consistency only — settled statements AND the
un-walked queue checked against each other. If the user declares
mid-walk (the contradiction catch often forces the realization),
update the preamble (only statements freeze; the preamble doesn't)
and re-check settled statements against the declared story, fixing
wording under their frozen numbers. Read whatever files exist and cite them
in the statements; facts recorded there count as supplied — no fresh
confirmation unless stale. When a VOTERS.md exists, the positioning
LEADS with its one or two voters — they are the claims your headline
statements should assert and prove — and it deliberately deploys the
special strengths as secondary supporting claims rather than ignoring
them; absent the file, work from the strongest true differentiator the
user names. If missing, say plainly — once, without
nagging in conversation (the file's Next-steps re-mention of the
fuller path is by design, not nagging) — that the rewrite will be
materially better with them (companion skills from this method's
author produce these, if installed: an ideal-customer method ending
in `asb-carol-define`, `asb-needs-stack` for the ladder, and
`asb-voters` for the voters / special-strengths file), then
proceed: elicit the minimum inline — the ideal customer by
circumstance and behavior (two lines), their scoreboard currency,
your level and one up/down — BEFORE diagnosing or rewriting
anything, since every gate leans on that context; caveat the file
accordingly.

### The closing press

Before finalizing, attack the whole statement list once. If a
devil's-advocate interrogation skill is installed in the environment
(for example *Rude Q&A* / `asb-rude-qa`, from the same author as this
method), invoke it against the draft file with this brief: *attack
these positioning statements — find every generic word and cliché
that slipped through; every claim whose opposite no one would
rationally choose; every save-money framing that survived; every
specific number or testimonial not confirmed true by the user; every
promise more than one level above what the product does; every claim
that contradicts the declared pricing strategy — premium-craft words
in a smart-choice story, affordability leads in a best story, hidden
shame about the weaknesses a deal story should own; every statement
any competitor could paste onto their own site unchanged.*
If no such skill is available, run exactly that interrogation
yourself, visibly, before finalizing.

## How to use this skill

### Phase A — Ingest

Collect the copy to convert (pasted or a file): headlines, ads,
homepage sections, pitch lines, positioning bullets. Then the context
ask (once, per the posture). If a `POSITIONING.md` already exists at
the target location, read it first: an in-progress header means
resume — pick up at the message the pointer names, don't re-litigate
settled statements, and don't repeat the context ask when the
preamble already records what exists (or that nothing does); a
finalized file means ask whether to extend or revise. Output lives
in the same directory as the input file(s); with pasted copy and no
files, ask where the method's files should live before creating
anything (default: the current directory).

Open small: what was read, the customer and level context in hand,
which messages look weakest and why — then message one.

### Phase B — Walk the messages

Per message, run the gates in this order (each visible, briefly):

1. **Level fit** — which level is this claim at, and is that the
   right role for it? (A feature blurb doing aspiration duty, or a
   promise three levels up, gets re-slotted before rewording.)
2. **Value framing** — is it save-time/save-money/efficiency? Reframe
   into the buyer's scoreboard currency.
3. **Price-story fit** — does the claim tell the declared strategy's
   story? Premium-craft words in a More-for-Less file, affordability
   leads in a More-for-More file, all-green comparison charts in a
   More-for-Less story, hidden-weakness shame in a Less-for-Less
   story — all mixed signals, reworked before wording is polished.
   (Strategy undeclared: check the statements against each other.)
4. **Vivid pass** — generics out, clichés out, show-don't-tell,
   compress for density.
5. **Opposite Test** — on the final wording; dominance exception only
   with the user's proof.
6. **Truth check** — every specific confirmed or bracketed
   `[VERIFY: …]` and listed.

Then record the survivor: statement text, its role, provenance
("reworked from: <original>"), citations to [N]/[K]/[D]/[E] numbers
where the files exist, and — when the sale has more than one head —
an audience tag ("for the finance approver") on any statement aimed
at someone other than the primary customer. **Create `POSITIONING.md` at the first settled
statement and append as you go** — copying the user's ENTIRE
remaining message queue into the `## Message queue` section verbatim
at creation, striking each message as it settles: original copy that
lives only in chat dies with the session. Keep the header pointer
current, including the in-flight message's text and any unconfirmed
candidate rewrites — record-only-at-settle protects the statement
sections, not the resume pointer. Landing a [VERIFY] fact later
fills the placeholder in place under the frozen P-number (completing
a placeholder isn't a revision) and marks the verify line landed.
The file is the memory, not the chat; if files aren't accessible,
re-emit the full draft in a fenced block every statement or two.

**Fill the gaps.** After the user's messages are done, check all
five roles plus commiseration: if nothing brags about the obviated
levels, counters the vendors above, references the far-above levels
as aspiration, or commiserates through an inciting event, propose
drafting those now — the point is a complete internal-reference
list. Gap-fill keeps the walk's pacing and rules (one statement per
exchange, candidates offered, facts pressed); the header tracks it
by role, and gap-fill doesn't increment the messages-settled count.
Two edge cases: with no vendor above, counter the nearest adjacent
occupant or skip the role with a note — don't invent an enemy; and a
commiseration *story* isn't a strategic claim — its gate is
recognition (would the ideal customer see themselves in it?) plus
provenance, marked observed (a real customer's story) or
hypothesized (a secondhand pattern, to validate before heavy spend).

### Phase C — Sweep and close

- **The closing press** (delegated or self-run, per the posture).
- **Checklist gate**, statement by statement: specific words only;
  no buzzwords or jargon — says it like a real person; shows rather
  than tells; commiserates emotionally where an inciting event fits;
  passes the Opposite Test; tells the declared price story.
- **Facts to verify surfaced**: read back with a plain warning that
  statements carrying `[VERIFY]` are not publishable until the real
  figures land.
- Finalize: remove the in-progress header and message queue; close
  by naming the reuse pattern — these statements are the internal
  reference for ad hooks, social topics, homepage claims, and sales
  scripts — and, where upstream files (or the pricing strategy) were
  missing, note that supplying them and re-running the weakest
  statements is the method's fuller path.

### The file structure

```markdown

> on: <the message, quoted — or "gap-fill: <role>"> <with any
> candidate rewrites offered but not yet confirmed>. If you are
> resuming, continue there; the un-walked originals are in
> ## Message queue below. (This note and the queue are removed at
> finalization.)

<Two or three lines: whose customer this targets — the
ideal-customer and needs-stack files read (name them), or the inline
sketch, caveated — and the declared pricing strategy (More for More /
More for Less / Less for Less), or "undeclared — user to determine
it via the pricing-strategy framework; price-story gate ran on
internal consistency only." Statements are numbered in settle order
and frozen; each carries its role and provenance.>

## What you promise (one level up)

**P1.** <statement>    [N3; K1]
    Reworked from: "<original message>"

## What you do (your level — features)

**P2.** <…>    [N1]
    Reworked from: "<…>"

<Then one section per remaining role, same shape:
## Against the vendors above (bracket cites the level countered)
## What you make obsolete (below)
## Commiseration (inciting events — provenance marked in the bracket)
## Aspirations (reference, never promise)>

## Message queue (originals, not yet walked — removed at finalization)

3. "<original message text, verbatim>"
4. "<…>"

## Facts to verify

- [VERIFY: <the fact>] — needed by P2. <who/where to get it>
- ~~[VERIFY: <…>]~~ — landed: <the confirmed fact>, filled into P1.

## Next steps

<Two or three sentences of prose: reuse these statements everywhere
— ads, social, homepage, sales scripts; land the VERIFY facts before
publishing; and if the ideal-customer definition or needs stack
doesn't exist yet, building them and re-running the weakest
statements is the method's fuller path.>
```

P-numbers are stable once written; revisions append new statements
(marking the old superseded) rather than renumbering. Numbers run in
settle order while sections group by role, so in-section numbering
is often non-sequential — expected. A counter statement's bracket
cites the level it counters. ("Solution" is banned in its
marketing-speak sense only.)

## Refusal conditions

- **Inventing specifics — never.** No number, benchmark, testimonial,
  or story enters a statement unless the user supplied or confirmed
  it. Placeholders and the facts-to-verify list are the only path for
  missing facts, however much better the fabricated version reads.
- **Generic words and clichés, however insisted.** "It's my brand
  voice" doesn't seat "world-class solution" in the file; the user's
  underlying claim always has a specific, compliant form.
- **Promises more than one level up.** "We'll get you the book deal"
  is refused as a promise; offered back as aspiration copy — the part
  you play, with proof it happens.
- **Save-money framing when a value framing exists.** Pressed and
  reframed into the buyer's scoreboard. (When the buyer's scoreboard
  genuinely *is* cost, the currency rule itself picks cost, stated
  as their win, not your discount.)
- **Mixed price signals, however insisted.** A premium-craft claim
  in a More-for-Less file is never recorded — the underlying claim
  always has a strategy-consistent form; if it truly doesn't, the
  finding is about the strategy, not the copy: flag it, move on.
- **Wholesale ghost-writing.** Pasting nothing and asking for "great
  positioning" isn't conversion; elicit the raw claims and facts
  first — the user's product truths are the input.
- **Building the needs stack or the ideal-customer definition.**
  Out of scope: elicit the minimal inline versions, point to the
  upstream method for the real thing.
- **Claims about products the user hasn't built.** Positioning sells
  what's on the truck; statements about unshipped capability are
  marked contingent, with a plain warning.
