# Keystones: Who Needs an Extreme Version of What You're Best At

Passionate purchase decisions don't come from sort-of liking a few
features. They come from an overwhelming pull toward one of your
strengths — something specific about the customer's circumstances that
makes them think "I've got to make this work for us." That pull is a
**keystone**, and mapping your keystones is how a strengths chart
becomes a target market. This skill walks the strengths one at a time,
generates keystone candidates with the user, and holds each to the
gate that separates keystones from wishful thinking: a real market
segment must typify it.

## The mental model

### What a keystone is

A keystone is the critical characteristic that drives a customer's
desire for the product — the thing that grabs their attention even in
a noisy world, even while other trade-offs are in flux. It is not the
strength itself; it is the *customer circumstance* that makes the
strength critical. "Fast website" is a strength. "Has a website where
even 0.1 seconds faster directly results in more revenue" is a
keystone — and it immediately names its market: e-commerce stores,
where studies put conversion of fast sites at multiples of slow ones,
and where the giants have measured revenue loss per tenth of a second
of load time. Same pattern across a real keystone chart: "scalable"
becomes *has traffic spikes at unpredictable times* (news sites whose
story can break any moment); "secure" becomes *just got hacked and is
still terrified* or *has a legal mandate for vendor certifications*
(the regulated industries); "inexpensive to build on" becomes *big
plans, limited budget* (every small business, every "do more with
less" marketing department).

### The generative question

For each strength, ask: **what characteristics, behaviors, or
circumstances REQUIRE an extreme version of this strength?** Imagine
the customer for whom this strength is absolutely critical to their
strategy — or so delightful it pulls them in through pure joy. Require
is the operative word: a circumstance that merely *prefers* the
strength generates a trade-off shopper — a buyer who weighs your
strength against a competitor's and picks whoever wins the spreadsheet
— not a keystone that drives a sale by itself. (Trade-off shoppers are
a fine, large audience; the method's point is that targeting the
keystone customer is what wins them too.)

### The gate: no segment, no keystone

After naming a keystone candidate, describe real-world examples of
companies or people who embody it. If you cannot name a significant
market segment that typifies the keystone, it isn't a keystone — the
strength is probably table stakes: true, useful, not compelling
enough to drive a purchase alone. That's fine — that's why it's
called brainstorming — but the candidate is cut, on the record, not
recorded hopefully. The segments named should be described by
circumstance and behavior ("e-commerce stores where speed measurably
moves revenue"), not raw demographics; the sharper the description,
the more useful every downstream step becomes.

### Keystones concentrate in the head, not everywhere

You won't find keystones by asking what your best customers have in
common — most of that is common to *all* your customers, including
the worst. A trait that describes everyone predicts nothing about
buying, paying, or staying. The right keystones concentrate in the
best customers and show up weakly, or not at all, in the bad ones. If
the strengths chart carries a best-vs-worst differential, mine it: the
differences are where keystones live.

### The one-keystone verdict

If the walk ends with one keystone — or none — that is not a failed
exercise; it is the most valuable strategic lesson available: **the
product isn't compelling yet.** Say it plainly, because learning it
now is a multiplier — fixing it increases sales, and it was hidden.
The twofold response, from the source: (a) increase marketing spend in
the markets where keystones DO exist (with zero keystones, this half
is empty and the verdict says so), and (b) use market and customer
research to determine what to build next that would address a real
keystone. Don't pad the chart to avoid the verdict — and don't pick
what to build yourself: a feature chosen from inside the strengths
chart is an invented keystone, exactly the wishful thinking the gate
kills. With one survivor, phrase it precisely: compelling to that one
segment only.

### Why this is worth doing at all

The customers keystones find are the head of the profit distribution —
in the canonical case, roughly 5% of customers producing 40% of
profit, which justifies spending many times more to acquire each one.
The keystone chart is the map to exactly those customers.

### Vocabulary

- **Keystone (K1, K2, …)** — a customer characteristic, behavior, or
  circumstance that requires an extreme version of a strength; cites
  its [S-number].
- **Example segments** — the real-world companies or people who
  typify the keystone; the gate every keystone must pass.
- **Table stakes** — a true, useful strength that compels no one by
  itself; recorded as a cut, not a keystone.
- **Requires vs. prefers** — the bar: a keystone circumstance makes
  the strength critical, not merely nice.

## The refiner's posture

### Generate freely; gate ruthlessly

Keystone-finding is brainstorming — propose candidates yourself,
liberally, alongside the user's: the imaginative leap from "strength"
to "whose circumstance requires this" is exactly where an outside
perspective helps. But every candidate, yours or theirs, faces the
same two tests before recording: does the circumstance *require* the
extreme version (not merely benefit from it), and can a significant
real segment be named that typifies it? Run both visibly. A
predefined way to press harder: if a devil's-advocate interrogation
skill is installed in the environment (for example *Rude Q&A* /
`asb-rude-qa`, from the same author as this method), invoke it
against the draft keystone chart with this brief: *attack these
keystones — find every one whose segment is vague, tiny, or invented;
every one where the circumstance merely prefers the strength rather
than requiring it; every segment described so broadly it describes
everyone. Don't accept wishful or hand-wavy defenses.* If no such
skill is available, run that interrogation yourself, visibly — the
per-candidate tests inline, the batch attack at the closing sweep.

### Press segment descriptions into specifics

"Small businesses" is not a segment description; "e-commerce stores
processing enough revenue that a measurable conversion lift pays for
premium hosting" is. When the user names a vague segment, ask for the
example companies — actual names, or a description sharp enough that
a salesperson could qualify against it. Generic words (many, serious,
growing, quality-focused) get pressed into numbers, behaviors, and
circumstances. A keystone whose segment can't be made specific after
pressing fails the gate.

### The user owns the market knowledge

Whether a named segment is significant, whether those customers
really exist, whether the circumstance actually occurs — the user
knows their market and their call stands, after one honest press
("you said the head of your customer base is 3+-location groups; this
keystone describes single-location shops — who's it for?"). What
their call cannot do is skip the gate: a keystone with no segment
attached doesn't enter the file no matter whose idea it was.

### One strength per exchange

Walk the strengths in order, one per exchange; a strength may yield
zero, one, or several keystones, and candidate variants within one
strength are one exchange's work. The opening move is small: what was
read, which strengths look most keystone-rich, then the first
strength. Compress ceremony on request, never structure.

## How to use this skill

### Phase A — Ingest

Read the strengths chart (default: `STRENGTHS-WEAKNESSES.md` in the
current directory; or pasted). Both-classified attributes participate
on their strength reading — the weakness half belongs to the next
step. Note the best-vs-worst differential if the chart (or its
observations file, if present) carries one; it seeds keystone
candidates. If no strengths chart exists, don't refine from nothing:
offer the on-ramp — run the classification step first (if a
classification skill from this method's author is installed, for
example *Strengths & Weaknesses* / `asb-carol-strengths`, name it),
or capture a defensible strengths list in chat now with the caveat
that unvetted strengths yield unvetted keystones. For the quick
capture: hold each claimed strength to a mini genericness test (would
any competitor claim the same words? then press for the mechanism or
the measured gap behind it), assign S-numbers as they settle, grab
the worst-customer picture too (the closing concentration check needs
it), and create the KEYSTONES.md immediately — captured strengths
recorded in its preamble, caveat included — so a died session loses
nothing and a resume finds the strengths on disk. Note in Next steps
that no weaknesses were captured, so the deal-breakers step must
capture or classify them first. If a KEYSTONES.md
already exists at the target location, read it: in-progress header
means resume — pick up at the strength the header names, don't
re-litigate settled keystones. Complete means ask whether to revise.

Output: `KEYSTONES.md` in the same directory as the input file. If the input was pasted and no path is known, ask where the method's files should live before creating anything (default: the current directory) — never scatter files silently.

### Phase B — Walk the strengths

Open small: what you read, which strengths look keystone-rich, any
best-vs-worst differential worth mining — then the first strength.
Per strength:

1. **Ask the generative question** — who requires an extreme version
   of this? Offer your own candidates alongside the user's; the
   best-vs-worst differences are prime material.
2. **Test each candidate**: requires-not-prefers, then the segment
   gate — name the real-world examples. Press vague segments into
   specifics.
3. **Record survivors** with [S-number] references and their example
   segments. A *strength* that yields no keystone is recorded in the
   file's table-stakes section with one line of reasoning; a failed
   *candidate* under a strength that did yield keystones dies in
   chat with its reasoning — no file entry needed.
4. Move to the next strength.

**Record to the file as you go.** Create `KEYSTONES.md` at the first
settled keystone; append after each; keep the header pointer current
(strengths walked, current strength). The file is the memory, not
the chat. If files aren't accessible, re-emit the full draft in a
fenced block every strength or two.

### Phase C — Sweep and close

- **Concentration check** — for each keystone: would this show up
  weakly or not at all in your *worst* customers? A keystone that
  describes everyone is re-tested and usually dies; one that's
  partially present in the worst survives with a "prioritize for
  honing" flag — the deal-breakers step tightens it.
- **Segment reality check** — the batch press (delegated or self-run,
  per the posture): segments real, significant, specific.
- **Count the survivors.** Two or more solid keystones: proceed. One
  or zero: deliver the verdict plainly — the product isn't compelling
  yet — with the twofold response recorded in the file (spend where
  keystones exist; research what to build that addresses a real
  keystone). This is a finding to act on, not a failure to soften.

Finalize: remove the in-progress header and close with the handoff.
The next step mirrors this one on the weaknesses — deal-breakers and
the anti-market — and then *hones these keystone segments* by
appending the qualifiers the deal-breakers force; if a deal-breakers
skill from this method's author is installed (for example
*Deal-Breakers* / `asb-carol-dealbreakers`), name it: "when you're
ready, run `asb-carol-dealbreakers` — it works on the weaknesses and
then sharpens this file's segment descriptions."

### The file structure

```markdown
# Keystones — <company / project name>

> ⚠️ IN PROGRESS — the walk is not complete. Strengths walked: <which
> S-numbers>; currently on: <S-number>. If you are resuming, continue
> there. (This note is removed at finalization.)

<Two or three lines: which strengths chart this refines (name it) and
the company context carried over. Keystones are customer
circumstances that REQUIRE an extreme version of a strength; each
names the real-world segments that typify it. Segment descriptions
here are pre-honing — the deal-breakers step appends qualifiers.>

## Keystones

**K1.** <The circumstance that requires the extreme version — specific,
behavioral.>    [S1]
    Example segments: <real-world companies/people who typify it —
    named or described sharply enough to qualify against.>

**K2.** <…>    [S3]
    Example segments: <…>

## Table stakes (true, but compels no one — cut)

- <Strength> — no significant segment requires an extreme version:
  <one line of reasoning>.    [S2]

## Verdict (only when one or zero keystones survive)

<The plain statement — the product is compelling only to <the one
segment>, or to no one yet — plus the twofold response: (a) spend
where the keystone exists, (b) research what to build that would
address a real keystone.>

## Next steps

<Two or three sentences of prose: do the mirror work on the
weaknesses — deal-breakers and the anti-market segments they
disqualify — then use those deal-breakers to hone each keystone
segment above with qualifiers ("…processing at least $X/mo," "…already
built on Y"). That's the next step of the method, and it works
directly from this file and the strengths chart.>
```

K-numbers are stable once written — later steps cite them and the
deal-breakers step edits this file's segment lines under them. Never
renumber.

## Refusal conditions

- **No strengths chart.** Keystones derived from unvetted strengths
  inherit their vagueness. Offer the classification step or a quick
  defensible capture first, caveated.
- **A keystone with no segment, however insisted.** The gate is the
  method: no significant real-world segment typifies it = table
  stakes, recorded as a cut. The refusal is of recording it as a
  keystone, never of the strength — which stays true and useful, just
  not compelling alone.
- **Vague segments.** "SMBs," "growing companies," "people who care
  about quality" — pressed into circumstance and behavior until a
  salesperson could qualify against the description, or the keystone
  fails the gate.
- **Padding to avoid the verdict.** When one or zero keystones
  survive, that verdict is the deliverable. Inventing weak keystones
  to make the chart look healthy defeats the exercise and poisons
  every downstream step.
- **Picking the target segment.** Which keystone market to pursue is
  strategy, made later with deal-breakers, inciting events, and the
  full definition in hand. This file maps the options; it doesn't
  choose.
- **Deal-breakers or the anti-market.** The weakness side is the next
  step's job — including the honing of this file's segments.
