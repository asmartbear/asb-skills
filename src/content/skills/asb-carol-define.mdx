## What this is about

"Women over forty-five" was the demographic profile of a certain
beloved sitcom's audience — and it missed that the show's fastest
growing fans were young men who loved sharp, character-driven insult
comedy. Demographics usually describe your market the same way: close
enough to feel right, wrong enough to waste every dollar aimed with
them. The book's alternative is to define Carol — your ideal customer —
by the circumstances, behaviors, and attitudes that actually predict
buying, paying, and staying: not "WordPress users above $1M revenue"
but *a person for whom a faster website directly increases revenue, and
who can calculate the dollar value of a hundred milliseconds.*

This is the capstone of the find-your-Carol workshop. It reads
everything the previous steps produced — classified strengths and
weaknesses, honed keystones, deal-breakers and the anti-market, and
inciting events — and synthesizes the crisp definition, pressing every
marker through the keep-asking-why chain (speed matters — to whom?
why?) until it clears the bar: Marketing can target it, Sales can
qualify against it, Product can build features to thrill it. Where the
keystone segments genuinely diverge, it makes you face the choice
instead of averaging them into nobody. And the file it writes leads
with the definition — because that's the point — followed by brief
reference sections on the keystones, deal-breakers, and inciting
events, because those details are exactly what the positioning,
marketing, and feature work downstream will consume.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-carol-define All four files are done — strengths, keystones
(honed), deal-breakers, inciting events. Write our Carol.
```

The skill drafts the complete CAROL.md — definition first, reference sections below — then reviews it with you section by section, refusing demographic laziness and generic markers until every line is something your team can act on.

One practical note: say where you're working. Name a directory (or
point at your existing files) when you invoke, and the skill keeps the
method's files together there — if you don't, it asks before creating
anything.

## From the source

Two sources form the foundation of this skill:

- As in Chapter 3 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *Speaking Carol Into Existence* (sections "Beyond Demographics" and "Meet Carol") — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — the behavioral definition standard, the keep-asking-why chain, the qualifier/disqualifier questions, and the five-step summary this capstone assembles.
- As in Chapter 2 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *Carol Loves You for You* — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — the Carol/Diana/Eddie bullseye: why targeting only Carol wins a market ten to one hundred times her size, which is the strategic case the definition file states.

Supporting sources each supply a piece of the mechanism:

- [**Selling to Carol: Why targeting an ICP brings 10x more customers than you expected**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/icp-ideal-customer-persona/) — the published form of the whole argument this file operationalizes.
- [**"It's a Balance" isn't always the answer**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/conflicting-choices/) — why diverging keystone segments force a choice, not an average: a Carol defined as the middle of two segments describes nobody.
- [**Specificity: A weapon of mass effectiveness**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/specificity/) — the wording bar for every marker: vague definitions can't be targeted, qualified, or built for.
- [**Choosing a target market**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/target-market/) — the menu of non-demographic dimensions (decision cycle, problem ownership, emergency-vs-deliberate, and two dozen more) that a candidate marker is tested against and extended from; demographics are the axes to avoid, these are the ones that predict buying.
