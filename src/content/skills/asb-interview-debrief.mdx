## What this is about

An interview you don't write down is an interview you'll misremember —
confirmation bias will quietly keep the parts you liked. The book's method
keeps a spreadsheet: one row per question, one column per interview. This
skill is that discipline without the spreadsheet: after each conversation,
it takes whatever raw material you have — a transcript, hasty notes — and
distills it into a brief, structured debrief file: one concise answer per
interview question (skipped honestly when you never got there), plus an
addenda section for everything interesting that fit no question, which is
often where the biggest learning hides.

The debrief is deliberately brief — evidence, not prose — and it accumulates:
one file per conversation in a directory that becomes your record of what
real customers actually said, ready to be synthesized into hypothesis
updates as the interviews stack up.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-interview-debrief Here's the transcript from my call with a gym owner
this morning — file it against my QUESTIONS.md.
```

The skill reads the transcript alongside your questions and drafts the
whole debrief: a short header on who this was and how the conversation
came about, then one brief answer per question actually asked — your
customer's key phrases kept verbatim, questions you never reached marked
as skipped rather than guessed at — each answer tagged with the hypotheses
it bears on, with a one-line commentary only where something you knew in
the moment will help the later analysis. Then it turns to you for what the
paper can't supply: where the transcript is garbled or ambiguous it asks
which reading is right, and it asks what surprised you that never made it
into the notes, because your memory of the conversation is a second
source. When the gym owner lectured you about what "most people" would
pay, that answer is kept but flagged — people are experts on their own
life, not on the market. Everything interesting that fit no question lands
in an addenda section, which is often where the biggest learning hides.
You confirm the draft, and the debrief joins your interviews directory:
one brief file per conversation, ready to be synthesized when the
interviews stack up.
