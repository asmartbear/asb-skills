The hardest part of a hard decision is that you cannot interrogate yourself.
Denial and rationalization are the default human operating mode, which is why
strategy documents are full of optimism and confirmatory data instead of the
scary challenges actually facing the business. The way through is an outside
attacker — someone willing to ask the unfair questions, refuse fluffy
answers, and stay on a point until you've earned a real answer.

This skill turns Claude into that attacker. Bring it anything you're
attached to and uncertain about — a decision, a plan, a pitch, a target
market, a pricing scheme, an idea still forming — as a one-line dilemma or a
fully-supported brief. Claude will grill it from every angle, including the
unfair ones, dwell wherever your answers go fuzzy, and refuse to wrap up
with "approximately fine." You walk away with one of three honest outcomes:
a sharpened plan made of real decisions with consequences explicitly
accepted, a clear list of what you need to gather or decide before the plan
can be defended, or the admission that it was the wrong idea after all.

The exercise is "heavy bat" practice — swinging against attacks harder than
reality will deliver, so that reality feels easy. The questions are
deliberately rude; the framing around them is collegial. The final output is
a markdown document capturing the decisions, the reasoning, the consequences
you accepted, and where you were uncomfortable along the way.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-rude-qa I'm writing a book which is outlined in the file
/Users/jcohen/book/Hidden Multipliers. In that file you can also see who
my audience is and why they should be interested in it. I don't know
exactly who my ICP is or how to market it.
```

After reading your outline, the interrogation goes after the soft spots
hard. It presses on whether your named audience actually cares about
these topics enough to buy and read another book — and stays on the
point if your answer is "anyone who runs a business" or "founders who
love thinking deeply." It asks how big the addressable market really
is, in numbers, and refuses your hand-waving. It points out the many
already-popular authors and books covering adjacent concepts — naming
specific ones — and demands a present-tense, specific differentiator
that isn't generic ("I write more clearly than them" fails; "the book is
structured around a previously-undocumented mechanism that none of the
others address" passes if you can name the mechanism and defend it). It
dwells on the ICP question you admitted you don't know, offering
candidate ICPs derived from your outline so you have something to react
to rather than a blank prompt. By the end, you walk away with either a
sharpened launch plan grounded in a defensible ICP and differentiation
story, a clear list of the specific things you need to find out before
the book can be marketed, or — in the hard case — the honest conclusion
that the book as currently scoped isn't differentiated enough to warrant
the launch effort.

## From the source

Two articles form the foundation of this skill:

- [**Rude Q&A: The constructive devil's advocate**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/devils-advocate/) — the technique itself, including why unfair, hostile, even erroneous questions are a feature rather than a flaw, and why dissatisfaction with your own answer is the signal the exercise is working.
- [**Failure to face the truth**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/failure-to-face-the-truth/) — the philosophical scaffolding underneath: why denial and rationalization are the default human operating mode, and why an outside attacker is the only reliable way through. Rude Q&A is the operational answer to the question this article poses.

Supporting articles each cover a piece of the mechanism or the target the interrogator is reaching for:

- [**What makes a strategy great**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/great-strategy/) — defines "Candid" and "Decisive" as non-negotiable qualities of strategy. Those qualities are the target state the interrogation drives toward.
- [**Strategic choices: When both options are good**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/strategic-choices/) — what a *real* decision looks like (both sides smart, consequences accepted, mutually reinforcing). That's the conclusion phase of the exercise.
- [**The "Opposite Test"**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/opposite-test/) — the specific stress-test the interrogator uses to detect meaningless claims and force them into real choices.
- [**Scars**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/scars/) — why even highly introspective operators cannot fix their blind spots alone, which is the deepest reason this skill needs to exist at all.
