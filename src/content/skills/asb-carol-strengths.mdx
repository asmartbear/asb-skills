## What this is about

A pile of honest observations isn't yet a strategy input — it's
evidence waiting for a verdict. This skill runs the two moves that turn
it into one. First, **distill**: multiple observations usually point at
one deeper truth ("customers post screenshots of support wait times"
becomes the attribute "slow tech support"), and a smaller set of
stronger attributes is easier to hold in mind and act on. Each
candidate attribute must survive the Opposite Test — if no successful
company would ever claim the opposite ("we love our customers"), the
attribute is banality, not strategy, and it doesn't get recorded.

Second, **classify** — and here the book resolves the paradox that
almost any attribute can be argued both ways. Whether something is a
strength or weakness is in the eye of the beholder, so the call is made
with a concrete rubric: it's a strength if more than a third of the
market sees it that way *or* some customers will buy for it alone; it's
a weakness if more than a third sees it that way *or* it makes the
product impossible for some customers, who will refuse for this reason
alone. Yes to both means it's *both* — recorded twice, and flagged,
because a both-attribute marks a place where market segments disagree
and a clear strategic choice is waiting. No to both means nobody cares:
the attribute is dropped, on the record, so you stop spending attention
on it. The result is the exact chart your keystones, deal-breakers, and
ultimately your ideal customer are derived from.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-carol-strengths Here's our OBSERVATIONS.md from the honest-look
exercise — 19 observations. Distill it down and tell me what we're
actually working with.
```

The skill proposes attributes one at a time — each citing the observations behind it, each grilled with the Opposite Test, each classified with you against the rubric — and records the results in `STRENGTHS-WEAKNESSES.md` as you go.

One practical note: say where you're working. Name a directory (or
point at your existing files) when you invoke, and the skill keeps the
method's files together there — if you don't, it asks before creating
anything.

## From the source

Two sources form the foundation of this skill:

- As in Chapter 2 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *Carol Loves You for You* (sections "Distill Into Important Attributes" and "Classify Strengths and Weaknesses") — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — the merge-or-drop distillation discipline, the genericness trap, and the two-question classification rubric this skill enforces.
- [**The "Opposite Test"**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/opposite-test/) — the genericness gate: a claim means something only if a successful company could rationally claim the opposite.

Supporting sources each supply a piece of the mechanism:

- [**Selling to Carol: Why targeting an ICP brings 10x more customers than you expected**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/icp-ideal-customer-persona/) — where these classified attributes are headed: the trade-offs Carol loves you for and everyone else weighs.
- [**"It's a Balance" isn't always the answer**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/conflicting-choices/) — why a both-classified attribute is a forced choice, not a compromise: when segments of the market disagree, making the clear choice is the essence of strategy.
- [**Specificity: A weapon of mass effectiveness**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/specificity/) — the wording bar attributes are held to: specific enough to create the edge that separates your ideal customer from everyone else.
