Ask a customer "Blogs get hacked all the time and it's devastating,
right? Would you like extra security?" and everyone says yes — they'd
look foolish otherwise. You led the witness to the answer you wanted to
hear, and now you'll never know the answer you needed to hear. Most
customer interviews fail exactly here: not in who you talk to, but in
questions that quietly telegraph the hoped-for answer and harvest polite
agreement dressed up as validation.

This is the third step of the interview method that runs goals →
hypotheses → questions. Because every question is anchored to a
hypothesis, writing good ones stops being guesswork: each question is a
miniature experiment, held to four criteria from the book — it must be
able to confirm or negate the hypothesis, hint at no particular answer,
elicit specifics rather than sentiment, and invite information you
didn't know to ask for. The skill drafts candidates, grills every
version — including yours — with a polite-stranger test, phrases
everything in your customers' own vocabulary, and refuses outright to
finalize a question that violates the criteria, because a leading
question doesn't just weaken an interview; it manufactures false
evidence. The one sanctioned exception, straight from the source: near
the end of an interview you may float a specific price, because the
visceral reaction to a real number is itself the data.

Give it your whole HYPOTHESES.md and it walks the list — grouping
related hypotheses so the set fits a real conversation, ordering the
result like an interview plan, and maintaining QUESTIONS.md as a live
file from the first settled question so a long session (or a resumed
one) never loses work. Or give it a single hypothesis and it simply
works that one question into shape with you and hands it back in chat —
no files, no ceremony.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-interview-questions Here's my HYPOTHESES.md from the hypotheses
exercise — turn these into the questions I'll actually ask.
```

The skill reads your hypotheses (and your goals file, for your
customers' vocabulary) and works through the list one question at a
time — proposing a merge when two hypotheses could share a question and
asking before combining, never dumping a batch of drafts on you at
once. For each question it drafts an open-ended candidate, attacks its
own draft
— could a polite stranger guess the answer you're hoping for? could an
honest answer fail to settle the hypothesis? — and iterates with you
until one survives. When you propose "So no-shows are a big problem for
you, right?" it names the tell, shows the open rewrite ("Walk me
through what happened the last time someone booked a class and didn't
show up"), and won't move on — or write the question down — until the
version on the table is genuinely unbiased. The finished QUESTIONS.md
reads like an interview plan: context-setting questions first, price
near the end, each question tagged with the hypotheses it tests, plus
standing probes for chasing surprises live.

Or bring one hypothesis with no file at all:

```text
/asb-interview-questions My hypothesis: gym owners spend at least 2
hours a week chasing failed membership payments. How do I ask about
this without planting the answer?
```

In this mode there's no file — the skill checks the hypothesis is sharp
enough to test, drafts and grills candidate phrasings with you, and
hands back the final question in chat, with a natural follow-up probe
to use when the answer surprises you.

Supporting articles each supply a piece of the mechanism:

- [**Yes, but who said they'd actually BUY the damn thing?**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/customer-validation/) — why "would you buy it?" questions produce worthless yeses, which is the failure mode the four criteria exist to prevent.
- [**When you have nothing: How to find potential customers to interview**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/find-customers-to-interview/) — where to find the people to ask, once the questions are ready.
