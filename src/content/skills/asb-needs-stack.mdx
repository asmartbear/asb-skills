## What this is about

Nobody's life ambition is to buy your product. The customer creating an
AWS account doesn't want infrastructure — they want the WordPress
website it enables. They don't really want the website either — they
want a personal platform for content and self-promotion, which they
want because they're chasing a book deal, which they want because…
there's always another level. That ladder is your customer's **Needs
Stack**, and the book's strategic insight is that a product satisfying
one level makes every product below it *obsolete* for that customer:
whoever delivers the website without the infrastructure wins the
customer, and the infrastructure vendor never even sees them.

Mapping your stack answers questions your competitor list can't: who
your real *alternatives* are (the vendors a level above you, quietly
removing customers from your market), what outcome you may honestly
promise (the level just above yours — features are what you do, the
next level up is what you promise), which steps you should brag about
making obsolete, which metric tells you whether customers actually
succeed, and where your company's higher purpose lives.

This skill builds the map interrogatively: it anchors the level your
product satisfies — phrased as your customer's goal in your customer's
words, not your product category — then walks down through what you
obviate and up through what they really want, one level per exchange.
Every level must pass a crystallization gate before the walk moves on:
specific wording, a true means-to-an-end link, real named occupants.
No sketching the whole ladder in one breath, and no stopping at your
own level — that's the trap the chapter warns about.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-needs-stack We sell managed Postgres hosting for small SaaS teams.
Here's our CAROL.md from the ideal-customer exercise — what does our
customer actually want, and who are our real alternatives?
```

The skill anchors your level in your customer's words, then walks the
stack down and up with you, recording each crystallized level — with
its occupants and its positioning role — in `NEEDS-STACK.md`.

## From the source

Two sources form the foundation of this skill:

- As in Chapter 4 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *A Rose by Any Other Name* (section "The Needs Stack") — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — the stack itself: the obviation rule, the shortest-path-vs-flexibility trade-off, the level-role framework this skill records (features at your level, promises one up, aspirations above, advancements below), and the purpose levels at the top.
- [**Using the Needs Stack for competitive strategy**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/needs-stack/) — the published deep treatment: alternatives vs. competitors, Google searches as a proxy for the level your customer is thinking at, measuring the next level up, and why climbing your own stack is usually a different business.

Supporting articles each supply a piece of the mechanism:

- [**How repositioning a product allows you to 8x its price**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/more-value-not-save-money/) — why the stack matters commercially: the level you position at determines the value you can charge for.
- [**Selling to Carol: Why targeting an ICP brings 10x more customers than you expected**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/icp-ideal-customer-persona/) — whose head you're climbing inside: the stack is built for your ideal customer, and diverges for anyone else.
