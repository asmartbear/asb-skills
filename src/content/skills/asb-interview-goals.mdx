## What this is about

You can't ask a customer "What should we charge?" or "Would you buy this
feature?" — the first gets a shrug and the second gets a polite "yes" that
evaporates when the invoice arrives. Yet pricing, positioning, and product
decisions are exactly what customer interviews are for. The way out of the
paradox is the first and most-skipped step of Jason's twenty-year-honed
interview method: writing down your **goal questions** — the questions you
need answered but cannot ask directly, numbered G1, G2, … so that every
hypothesis and interview question you write later traces back to one.

This skill interviews you about your business — new idea or twenty-year-old
company, B2B or B2C — then works with you to forge a goal-question list
tailored to your situation rather than a copy of anyone's template. When
the list is sharp, it writes GOALS.md: your context in prose, then the
numbered goal questions, preserved for the rest of the interview process.

## Example invocation

You can invoke the skill like this:

```text
/asb-interview-goals We run a four-year-old B2B SaaS for property managers,
about $40k MRR, mostly small firms. Growth stalled this year. We're debating
a price increase and whether to move upmarket, and honestly we've never done
real customer interviews.
```

The skill starts by interviewing you, a question or two at a time: what
stage the business is at, what decisions the interviews must inform, what
you already believe about pain and price, what prompted this now. It
presses where the stakes go fuzzy — "we just want to understand customers
better" isn't a decision — but it doesn't demand you already know who your
ideal customer is; discovering that is partly what the interviews are
*for*, so it becomes a goal question rather than a prerequisite. And it
won't accept goals that customers can't answer, like "Would they buy the
new reporting module?", reframing them into questions about the customer's
actual life that interviews *can* answer. Then it drafts a goal-question
list tailored to the two decisions you actually face (pricing and
upmarket), critiques its own draft against the framework's standard,
revises it with you, and writes the result to GOALS.md: your business
context in prose at the top, then the numbered goal questions G1, G2, …
ready to drive the hypotheses and interview questions that come next in
the method.

The skill works just as well from the opposite end of the spectrum — no
customers, no product, just an idea to validate:

```text
/asb-interview-goals My startup idea is an AI-powered phone system for
plumbers, to schedule appointments or diagnose simple issues while the
plumber is busy. Solo operators. Validating before I build anything.
```

Here the goal list comes out completely different, because the decision
at stake is "build it at all?" The goals aim at whether the pain even
exists and who has it worst: what actually happens when a solo plumber
misses a call mid-job, what a missed call costs, how they cope today
(voicemail, a spouse, an answering service, losing the job), what they
already pay for around the office side of the business, and what moment
would make one of them change how they answer the phone. Your hunch that
solo operators are the market is taken as the rough sketch it is —
sharpening it into a real ideal-customer definition is what the
interviews are for — and "would plumbers pay for an AI receptionist?"
gets kept off the list, because polite yeses to that question are how
unbuilt products end up built and unsold.

## From the source

Two sources form the foundation of this skill:

- As in Chapter 10 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *A multiplier of one's own* (section "How to Extract Insights from Customers") — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — the five-step interview method this skill's goal-questions step begins, including the canonical eleven-goal list for a typical B2B company and the WP Engine story of what the method unlocked.
- [**The Iterative-Hypothesis customer development method**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/customer-development/) — the published article version of the same method: goals → hypotheses → open-ended questions → iterate → stop when it's boring.

Supporting sources each supply vocabulary or motivation behind specific goal questions:

- [**Selling to Carol: Why targeting an ICP brings 10x more customers than you expected**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/icp-ideal-customer-persona/) — defines Carol, the ideal-customer persona several canonical goal questions are written about.
- As in Chapter 3 of *Hidden Multipliers*: *Speaking Carol Into Existence* — [hiddenmultipliers.com](https://hiddenmultipliers.com) — keystones, deal-breakers, inciting events, and identifying attributes: the concepts the "What does Carol look like?" family of goals is designed to uncover.
- [**Using the Needs Stack for competitive strategy**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/needs-stack/) — the higher-level outcomes a customer expects as a byproduct, the target of the final canonical goal question.
- [**Yes, but who said they'd actually BUY the damn thing?**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/customer-validation/) — why real customer evidence is non-negotiable and why "I'm my own customer" is a trap, i.e. why this exercise exists.
- [**When you have nothing: How to find potential customers to interview**](https://longform.asmartbear.com/find-customers-to-interview/) — where to find interviewees once your goals (and later, questions) are ready.
