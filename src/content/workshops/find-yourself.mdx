## What this is about

The right strategy — for a product, a company, a career — is the one whose
success hinges on things you already do exceptionally well and *want* to keep
doing for years. It's the game you're built to win: your King's Gambit, the
opening competitors dismiss as "refuted" because they're evaluating the wrong
player. But you can't build a company *in the shape of you* until you know the
shape — and "know thyself" has been the hardest assignment for two and a half
millennia. You can't read the label from inside the jar. You're biased, you
rehearse who you think you *should* be, and your most powerful traits are
invisible to you precisely because they come so naturally — like the extrovert
who works a room and genuinely can't explain "how."

This workshop — from *To Thine Own Self Be True*, Chapter 11 of
*[Hidden Multipliers](https://hiddenmultipliers.com)* — is the companion to
[Find Your Carol](/workshops/find-your-carol/). There you constructed the
customer who is naturally perfect for your product; here you construct the
strategy that is naturally perfect for *you*. It runs as two skills. The first
draws an honest map of who you are — recording each trait not as a strength or a
weakness but as a *fact* whose expression depends on context ("Competitive" wins
a sales call and poisons a team meeting). The second distills that map down to
the one or two traits so extreme, and so idiosyncratically yours, that customers
accept every other trade-off. Each produces a file the next step — and your
positioning, pricing, and product decisions — consumes.

## How to run it

1. **Map who you actually are.**
   [Who, Me?](/skills/asb-who-me/) runs the way out of the jar: the drive
   prompts (*even as a kid I would ___*), the anti-questions (*when I'm faced
   with ___, I feel it in the pit of my stomach*), and the outside-in questions
   you hand to people who know you — your perfect scenario, your personal hell,
   and the strengths you can't see because they come so naturally. It presses
   every flattering label for the concrete episode behind it and welcomes the
   unglamorous motives — money, fame, proving a point. The result is
   `WHO-ME.md`: each finding a fact of your personality, with the contexts where
   it helps and where it quietly wrecks things.
2. **Distill to your voter(s).**
   [Voters](/skills/asb-voters/) takes that self-portrait and runs every
   candidate through a hard gauntlet — extremity earned through obsession,
   rarity among peers, decisiveness, and reverberation into product, pricing,
   and market — then caps the answer at two. It's the debate tactic that wins a
   contest you're losing on points: one or two decisive strengths override every
   deficiency, the way Steve Jobs's obsession with industrial design overpowered
   iTunes. The output is `VOTERS.md`: at most two voters with their evidence and
   downstream consequences, plus the near-miss *special strengths* to build on as
   secondary pillars — or the honest verdict that nothing is extreme enough yet.

## What you get at the end

`VOTERS.md` is the seed of a strategy competitors can't copy, because it grows
from an obsession they aren't willing to match. It flows outward into the rest
of your decisions: [positioning](/skills/asb-positioning/) that leads with the
voter, a pricing strategy that lives up to it, a product that reverberates from
it, and a market that loves you *for* it — even reading your weaknesses as proof
of the thing they came for.

And it closes the loop with [Find Your Carol](/workshops/find-your-carol/):
when you and your product are in harmony but the customer isn't, then by
definition that wasn't Carol — you re-run the Carol analysis with what you now
know. Together, the two "Who?" workshops pin down the two people every strategy
turns on: your ideal customer, and you.
